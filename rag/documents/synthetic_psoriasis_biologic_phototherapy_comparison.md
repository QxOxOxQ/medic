## Abstract

### Background

Chronic plaque psoriasis is an immune-mediated inflammatory skin condition. Treatment pathways may include topical therapy, phototherapy, conventional systemic therapy, and biologic therapy. The choice of treatment depends on disease severity, lesion location, comorbidities, prior therapy response, patient preference, safety profile, pregnancy plans, infection risk, access, cost, and monitoring requirements.

### Objective

This synthetic study compared a step-up pathway beginning with narrow-band ultraviolet B phototherapy against an early biologic pathway in adults with moderate chronic plaque psoriasis. The fictional report was designed to test retrieval of treatment sequencing, response thresholds, safety monitoring, and patient-reported outcomes.

### Methods

A total of 240 fictional participants were assigned to either a phototherapy-first pathway or an early biologic pathway. Participants were followed for 32 weeks. The primary endpoint was the proportion achieving a 75% improvement in Psoriasis Area and Severity Index at week 16. Secondary endpoints included itch score, Dermatology Life Quality Index, treatment burden, flare frequency, and adverse events.

### Results

The early biologic pathway had a higher week-16 PASI 75 response rate. The phototherapy-first pathway had lower systemic immunomodulation exposure but higher visit burden. By week 32, some phototherapy-first participants escalated to systemic therapy because of inadequate response or relapse after treatment gaps.

### Conclusion

In this fictional dataset, early biologic therapy produced faster and higher skin response, while phototherapy-first reduced early systemic exposure but increased logistical burden. The report is intended for RAG testing, especially around nuanced comparisons and contraindication-like safety language.

## Background

### Disease Overview

Psoriasis is a chronic inflammatory disease that commonly presents with erythematous, scaly plaques. Symptoms can include itch, pain, cracking, bleeding, embarrassment, sleep disruption, and reduced quality of life. Moderate disease may still cause significant impairment when plaques affect visible areas, hands, feet, scalp, genitals, or occupationally relevant surfaces.

### Treatment Pathways

Treatment is often staged. Mild disease may be treated with topical corticosteroids, vitamin D analogues, keratolytics, or combination regimens. Moderate disease may require phototherapy or systemic therapy. Biologic agents target immune pathways and can produce strong efficacy, but they require screening, monitoring, access approval, and consideration of infection risk.

- PASI 75 versus PASI 90,

- phototherapy visit burden versus systemic exposure,

## Eligibility Criteria

### Inclusion Criteria

Participants were eligible if they met all of the following:

1. Adult age 18 to 70 years.
2. Chronic plaque psoriasis for at least 6 months.
3. Baseline PASI between 8 and 18.
4. Candidate for either phototherapy or systemic therapy.
5. Able to attend follow-up assessments.
6. No biologic therapy within the prior 12 months.
7. Stable topical regimen for at least 4 weeks.

### Exclusion Criteria

Participants were excluded for:

1. Active serious infection.
2. Recent malignancy requiring active treatment.
3. Photosensitivity disorder.
4. Inability to attend phototherapy visits.
5. Pregnancy or planned pregnancy during the study.
6. Severe erythrodermic or pustular psoriasis.
7. Uncontrolled inflammatory bowel disease.
8. Recent live vaccine.
9. Unreliable follow-up availability.
10. Contraindication to both study pathways.

### Screening

Participants in the early biologic pathway completed screening before treatment initiation. Screening included infection risk review, tuberculosis testing, hepatitis status review, vaccination review, medication history, and assessment for relevant comorbidities. This synthetic report does not provide real prescribing instructions.

## Interventions

### Phototherapy-First Pathway

Participants began narrow-band UVB phototherapy three times per week for up to 12 weeks. Dose adjustments were made according to erythema response and missed visits. Topical therapy could continue if stable.

Phototherapy was considered inadequate if:
- PASI improvement was below 50% by week 12,
- DLQI remained above 10 with persistent plaques,
- attendance dropped below 70% because of logistical burden,
- flare occurred after repeated treatment interruption.

Participants with inadequate response could escalate to systemic therapy after clinical review.

### Early Biologic Pathway

Participants began biologic therapy after screening completion. The synthetic protocol does not name a specific biologic class. The pathway represents early systemic immunomodulatory therapy rather than a particular drug.

Biologic response was assessed at week 16. Inadequate response was defined as:
- not achieving PASI 50 by week 16,
- persistent high-impact area disease despite partial PASI improvement,
- unacceptable adverse event,
- participant preference to discontinue.

### Concomitant Topical Therapy

Both groups could use stable topical therapy. Rescue topical therapy was allowed for localized flares but was recorded as rescue medication use.

## Treatment Escalation Rules

### Phototherapy Escalation

Escalation from phototherapy-first to systemic therapy was considered when:
- PASI 50 was not achieved by week 12,
- DLQI remained above 10,
- high-impact areas persisted,
- frequent missed visits prevented adequate exposure,
- patient preferred systemic therapy after shared review.

### Biologic Adjustment

Biologic adjustment was considered when:
- PASI 50 was not achieved by week 16,
- high-impact disease persisted,
- adverse events were unacceptable,
- infection risk changed,
- patient preference changed.

### Shared Decision-Making

Shared decision-making incorporated:
- disease severity,
- lesion location,

- work schedule,
- travel distance,
- infection risk,

## Subgroup Analyses

### High-Impact Area Disease

Participants with scalp, palmoplantar, genital, or visible facial involvement reported greater quality-of-life burden at baseline. Some participants with moderate total PASI had severe subjective impact because plaques were located in high-impact areas.

### Visit Attendance

Among phototherapy-first participants, attendance below 70% was associated with lower response and higher relapse. This association should not be interpreted as randomized evidence that attendance alone determines response, because attendance may correlate with work flexibility, distance, and disease motivation.

### Prior Systemic Therapy

Participants with prior systemic therapy had slightly lower response in both pathways. The sample was too small for strong conclusions.

## Discussion

The synthetic report demonstrates a common treatment tradeoff. Early biologic therapy delivered higher and faster skin clearance in this fictional dataset. Phototherapy-first avoided early systemic immunomodulation but required frequent visits and had lower week-16 response.

### Key Interpretation

The correct interpretation is not "biologics are always better." The correct interpretation is that early biologic therapy had higher response rates in this synthetic population, while phototherapy-first may be reasonable for selected patients who prefer to avoid systemic therapy and can attend frequent visits.

### RAG-Relevant Distinctions

A strong retrieval system should distinguish:
- PASI 75 from PASI 90,
- primary endpoint from secondary endpoints,
- week 16 response from week 32 durability,
- treatment efficacy from treatment burden,
- safety monitoring from adverse event incidence,
- escalation criteria from exclusion criteria.

4. "Phototherapy has no systemic risks, so it is always safer" — oversimplified.
5. "All patients escalated to biologics" — incorrect; 34% escalated by week 32.
