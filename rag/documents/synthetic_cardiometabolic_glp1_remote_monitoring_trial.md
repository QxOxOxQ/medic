## Abstract

### Background

Obesity, insulin resistance, and early type 2 diabetes are frequently managed using pharmacological treatment, dietary counseling, physical activity interventions, and longitudinal monitoring. In recent years, incretin-based therapies, especially glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists, have been studied for their effects on body weight, glycemic control, appetite regulation, and cardiometabolic markers. However, real-world outcomes may depend heavily on adherence, nutrition quality, gastrointestinal tolerance, medication titration, behavioral support, and monitoring intensity.

This synthetic study evaluates a combined intervention consisting of a GLP-1 receptor agonist, structured lifestyle coaching, and remote digital monitoring. The fictional trial was designed to test whether a medication-centered intervention performs differently when paired with high-frequency lifestyle feedback and biometric tracking.

### Objective

The primary objective was to compare change in body weight at 24 weeks between participants receiving GLP-1 therapy with standard lifestyle advice and participants receiving GLP-1 therapy with remote lifestyle monitoring.

Secondary objectives included changes in fasting glucose, HbA1c, waist circumference, systolic blood pressure, triglycerides, quality-of-life score, gastrointestinal symptom burden, and adherence to the planned dose escalation schedule.

### Methods

This was a randomized, open-label, parallel-group, synthetic trial involving 312 adult participants. Participants had overweight or obesity and at least one cardiometabolic risk factor. Participants were randomized to either standard care or remote monitoring. Remote monitoring included weekly nutrition logs, step-count summaries, body weight uploads, symptom check-ins, and monthly telehealth review.

### Results

At week 24, the remote monitoring group showed greater mean weight reduction than the standard care group. Improvements were also observed in waist circumference, fasting glucose, and self-reported dietary adherence. Gastrointestinal symptoms were common in both groups, especially during the first 8 weeks. The remote monitoring group had fewer unscheduled discontinuations, possibly because early symptom reporting allowed dose adjustment.

### Conclusion

In this fictional dataset, remote lifestyle monitoring increased adherence and improved selected cardiometabolic outcomes when combined with GLP-1 therapy. The study highlights the importance of separating pharmacological effect from implementation quality when designing RAG datasets involving clinical interventions.

## Background

### Clinical Context

Cardiometabolic disease exists on a continuum. Many patients present with central adiposity, elevated fasting glucose, dyslipidemia, hypertension, fatty liver markers, sleep disturbance, or low cardiorespiratory fitness before meeting formal diagnostic criteria for advanced disease. Interventions targeting weight loss may produce improvements across multiple metabolic endpoints, but magnitude varies by baseline risk, adherence, muscle mass, medication tolerance, and lifestyle context.

GLP-1 receptor agonists influence appetite, satiety, gastric emptying, and glycemic regulation. They are often associated with nausea, early satiety, constipation, diarrhea, reflux symptoms, and reduced food intake. In clinical practice, the ability to tolerate titration may determine whether a patient reaches an effective maintenance dose. A patient who discontinues therapy after early nausea may receive limited long-term benefit even if the pharmacological mechanism is strong.

### Need for Monitoring

Remote monitoring is not a drug. It is an implementation layer. It can provide feedback loops, identify intolerance, improve accountability, surface adverse events, and capture real-world adherence patterns. In RAG testing, this distinction matters because retrieval systems should correctly answer whether the intervention effect is attributed to the medication alone or to the combined medication-monitoring pathway.

### RAG Testing Relevance

This document intentionally contains overlapping concepts: weight loss, adherence, monitoring, safety, gastrointestinal symptoms, metabolic outcomes, and quality-of-life measures. A useful retrieval system should distinguish:
- medication effect from monitoring effect,
- primary endpoint from secondary endpoints,
- adverse events from treatment discontinuation,
- protocol-defined adherence from self-reported adherence,
- study limitations from clinical conclusions.

## Study Design

### Trial Design

This was a synthetic, randomized, open-label, parallel-group trial with two arms:

| Arm | Intervention | Monitoring Intensity | Visit Schedule |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Standard Care | GLP-1 receptor agonist + printed lifestyle advice | Low | Baseline, week 8, week 16, week 24 |
| Remote Monitoring | GLP-1 receptor agonist + digital monitoring + lifestyle coaching | High | Baseline, weekly digital check-in, week 8, week 16, week 24 |

The open-label design means participants and investigators were aware of group assignment. Outcome assessors were assumed to be blinded for laboratory endpoints, but not for self-reported adherence or quality-of-life questionnaires.

### Study Duration

The total duration was 24 weeks. A four-week screening window preceded randomization. Participants completed a baseline assessment, then underwent medication titration according to a predefined escalation plan. The remote monitoring group submitted weekly weight and symptom records.

### Randomization

Participants were randomized in a 1:1 ratio using a synthetic block randomization scheme stratified by baseline HbA1c category:
- HbA1c below 6.5%;
- HbA1c equal to or above 6.5%.

Randomization was not stratified by sex, baseline BMI, or prior weight-loss attempt history.

## Population

### Inclusion Criteria

Participants were eligible if they met all of the following criteria:

1. Age between 30 and 68 years.
2. Body mass index from 29.0 to 42.0 kg/m².
3. At least one cardiometabolic risk factor:
   - impaired fasting glucose,
   - type 2 diabetes managed without insulin,
   - elevated triglycerides,
   - treated hypertension,
   - increased waist circumference.
4. Ability to use a smartphone or web-based symptom diary.
5. Willingness to follow scheduled assessments.
6. No planned bariatric surgery during the study period.

### Exclusion Criteria

Participants were excluded for any of the following:

1. History of pancreatitis.
2. Severe gastrointestinal motility disorder.
3. Pregnancy or planned pregnancy during the study period.
4. Current insulin therapy.
5. Recent major cardiovascular event within 90 days.
6. Prior GLP-1 receptor agonist use within 12 months.
7. Inability to provide reliable remote monitoring data.
8. Participation in another interventional trial.

## Intervention

### Medication Protocol

Both groups received a GLP-1 receptor agonist using a four-step titration schedule. Dose escalation could be paused for gastrointestinal intolerance, persistent vomiting, or participant preference.

| Week | Planned Dose Level | Dose Adjustment Rule |
|---|---|---|
| 0-4 | Initiation dose | Continue unless severe intolerance |
| 5-8 | Low maintenance transition | Pause escalation if nausea score ≥ 7/10 |
| 9-16 | Intermediate dose | Reduce if persistent vomiting occurs |
| 17-24 | Target maintenance dose | Maintain unless discontinuation criteria met |

This synthetic protocol intentionally avoids naming a real medication or specific dose. It is designed to test retrieval of dose-escalation concepts without referencing actual prescribing instructions.

### Lifestyle Advice

The standard care group received a printed lifestyle guide covering:
- protein intake consistency,
- reduction of ultra-processed snacks,
- regular walking,
- resistance training twice weekly,
- sleep routine stabilization,
- hydration guidance,
- symptom diary recommendations.

### Remote Monitoring Intervention

The remote monitoring group used a fictional digital platform named **MetaboTrack-R**. The platform collected:

| Data Type | Frequency | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Body weight | Weekly | Track trend and adherence |
| Gastrointestinal symptoms | Weekly | Identify intolerance early |
| Step count | Weekly | Monitor activity trend |
| Nutrition log | 3 days per week | Estimate dietary adherence |
| Medication self-report | Weekly | Detect missed doses |
| Quality-of-life survey | Monthly | Track subjective burden |

Alerts were triggered when participants reported severe nausea, repeated vomiting, dizziness, inability to tolerate food, or rapid weight change above a predefined threshold. Alerts led to telehealth review, but not automatic medication changes.

## Statistical Methods

The primary analysis used an intention-to-treat population including all randomized participants. Missing week-24 weights were imputed using a conservative baseline-carried-forward method in a sensitivity analysis. A per-protocol analysis was also conducted for participants who completed at least 80% of required visits and submitted sufficient monitoring data.

Continuous outcomes were summarized as means and standard deviations. Between-group differences were estimated with synthetic linear models adjusting for baseline value and HbA1c category. Categorical outcomes were summarized as counts and percentages. No formal multiplicity correction was applied to secondary endpoints because this document is designed for RAG testing.

## Safety

### Adverse Events

Gastrointestinal adverse events were common in both groups.

| Adverse Event | Standard Care | Remote Monitoring |
|---|---:|---:|
| Nausea | 42% | 46% |
| Constipation | 24% | 28% |
| Diarrhea | 18% | 19% |
| Vomiting | 11% | 9% |
| Reflux symptoms | 14% | 16% |
| Dizziness | 7% | 8% |
| Serious adverse event | 2% | 1% |

Most gastrointestinal symptoms were mild or moderate. Severe symptoms occurred during titration and typically resolved after dose delay or supportive care. The remote monitoring group reported more nausea, which may reflect more frequent symptom capture rather than a true increase in symptom incidence.

### Discontinuation

Permanent discontinuation occurred in 15% of standard care participants and 8% of remote monitoring participants. In the fictional case narratives, standard care participants often delayed reporting symptoms until the next scheduled visit, whereas remote monitoring participants received earlier review.

### Safety Interpretation

The study does not prove that monitoring reduces adverse events. It suggests that monitoring may reduce permanent discontinuation by detecting intolerance earlier. This distinction is important for retrieval because a model might incorrectly answer that remote monitoring directly lowered adverse event rates. The more precise conclusion is that remote monitoring improved management of intolerance.

## Subgroup Analyses

### Baseline HbA1c

Participants with HbA1c equal to or above 6.5% had greater glycemic improvement than those below 6.5%. Weight loss patterns were directionally similar across HbA1c strata.

### Baseline BMI

Participants with baseline BMI above 35 kg/m² had slightly greater absolute weight loss in kilograms but similar percentage weight loss. This pattern is expected because heavier participants can lose more absolute weight for the same relative percentage.

### Monitoring Adherence

Within the remote monitoring group, participants completing at least 80% of weekly check-ins had greater average weight reduction than those completing fewer check-ins. This association should not be interpreted as causal because more adherent participants may also adhere better to nutrition and medication.

## Discussion

The synthetic trial demonstrates how implementation intensity can influence apparent medication outcomes. The medication was the same in both groups, but the monitoring layer changed adherence, symptom management, and participant engagement. The remote monitoring group had better completion, fewer discontinuations, and stronger weight-loss outcomes.

### Interpretation of Primary Outcome

The primary endpoint favored remote monitoring. However, the observed difference is not purely pharmacological. The effect likely reflects:
- better adherence,
- earlier management of side effects,
- more consistent lifestyle tracking,
- increased accountability,
- reduced loss to follow-up.

### Clinical Implementation Lessons

A production RAG system should retrieve the correct section when asked:
- "Which group lost more weight?"
- "Did monitoring reduce nausea?"
- "What was the primary endpoint?"
- "Was the study blinded?"
- "What explains fewer discontinuations?"
- "What were the limitations?"

### LLM Failure Modes

Potential hallucination risks include:

3. Confusing temporary dose pauses with discontinuation.
4. Treating subgroup associations as randomized causal findings.
5. Inventing a named commercial medication.
