## Abstract

### Background

Anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction is followed by a prolonged rehabilitation period. Some patients experience recurrent knee effusion, quadriceps inhibition, delayed strength recovery, reduced confidence, or difficulty returning to sport. Persistent swelling can complicate rehabilitation because joint effusion may inhibit quadriceps activation and alter movement mechanics.

### Objective

This synthetic study compared two rehabilitation pathways for patients with persistent mild-to-moderate knee effusion 3 to 9 months after anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction. The objective was to evaluate whether a criteria-based pathway with load management and swelling-guided progression improved quadriceps strength symmetry compared with a time-based standard pathway.

### Methods

A fictional cohort of 188 patients after ACL reconstruction was assigned to either standard time-based rehabilitation or a criteria-based swelling-guided pathway. The swelling-guided pathway adjusted training load according to effusion grade, pain response, range of motion, quadriceps activation, hop tolerance, and next-day symptom response.

### Results

At 16 weeks, the criteria-based pathway showed better quadriceps limb symmetry and fewer symptom flares. Return-to-run clearance occurred later in some participants but was associated with fewer regressions. Participants in the standard pathway advanced earlier by calendar time, but a subset developed recurrent effusion after plyometric or running progression.

### Conclusion

In this fictional report, swelling-guided rehabilitation improved strength recovery and reduced symptom flares. The document is designed to test retrieval of nuanced rehabilitation concepts, including the difference between time-based progression and criteria-based progression.

## Background

### Clinical Context

ACL reconstruction is commonly performed after rupture of the anterior cruciate ligament. Surgical reconstruction restores mechanical stability but does not automatically restore strength, neuromuscular control, confidence, or tissue tolerance. Rehabilitation is a staged process. Progression too slowly may delay function, while progression too quickly may provoke swelling, pain, altered mechanics, or compensatory movement.

Persistent effusion is clinically important because intra-articular swelling can reduce quadriceps activation through arthrogenic muscle inhibition. This means a patient may be willing to train but unable to recruit the quadriceps effectively. The result may be asymmetry, poor landing control, reduced knee extension confidence, or overload of the contralateral limb.

### Rationale for Criteria-Based Rehabilitation

Traditional rehabilitation sometimes uses time since surgery as the primary marker. For example, patients may start running at a particular month postoperatively. Criteria-based rehabilitation uses functional markers instead:
- minimal effusion,
- full or near-full extension,
- acceptable flexion,
- controlled pain,
- quadriceps strength symmetry,
- quality of single-leg squat,
- hop mechanics,
- response to previous session.

This distinction is important for RAG testing. A model should not answer that all patients can run at a fixed month. The document states that readiness depends on criteria, not only time.

## Study Design

### Overview

This was a synthetic prospective comparative rehabilitation study. Participants were 3 to 9 months after ACL reconstruction and had recurrent grade 1 or grade 2 knee effusion. The study compared:

| Group | Rehabilitation Strategy | Progression Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Standard Pathway | Calendar-based protocol | Time since surgery |
| Swelling-Guided Pathway | Criteria-based protocol | Effusion, pain, ROM, strength, symptom response |

### Study Duration

Participants were followed for 16 weeks. Assessments occurred at baseline, week 4, week 8, week 12, and week 16. Weekly symptom checks recorded swelling, pain, perceived instability, training response, and confidence.

### Population

A total of 188 participants were included.

LSI means limb symmetry index. A quadriceps LSI of 68% means the reconstructed limb produced 68% of the strength of the contralateral limb in the defined test.

## Eligibility Criteria

### Inclusion Criteria

Participants were eligible if they met all of the following:

1. ACL reconstruction performed 3 to 9 months earlier.
2. Recurrent mild-to-moderate knee effusion.
3. Ability to perform basic closed-chain strengthening.
4. Passive knee extension deficit no greater than 5 degrees.
5. No complete graft rupture suspected clinically.
6. Willingness to record next-day response after training.

### Exclusion Criteria

Participants were excluded for:

1. Revision ACL reconstruction.
2. Multiligament reconstruction.
3. Current infection.
4. Locked knee requiring urgent surgical assessment.
5. Severe extension loss.
6. Complex regional pain syndrome.
7. New acute trauma during screening.
8. Inability to follow rehabilitation instructions.

### Imaging and Clinical Assessment

Imaging was not repeated systematically for all participants. MRI was ordered selectively when participants had mechanical locking, sharp joint-line pain, progressive extension loss, suspected cyclops lesion, suspected meniscal pathology, or unexplained worsening effusion. The study did not claim that MRI is mandatory for every swollen knee after ACL reconstruction.

## Interventions

### Standard Time-Based Pathway

The standard pathway followed a predefined timeline:

| Time Window | Typical Focus |
|---|---|
| Weeks 1-4 | Strength base, bike, range of motion |
| Weeks 5-8 | Progressive resistance, low-amplitude plyometrics |
| Weeks 9-12 | Return-to-run exposure |
| Weeks 13-16 | Cutting drills and sport-specific progression |

Progression could be slowed by therapist judgment, but the default structure was based on calendar time.

### Swelling-Guided Criteria-Based Pathway

The swelling-guided pathway used objective and subjective criteria. Progression was allowed only when all required criteria were met.

#### Progression Criteria

| Criterion | Required Before Load Increase |
|---|---|
| Effusion | Grade 0 or stable grade 1 |
| Pain | ≤ 3/10 during session and next day |
| Range of motion | Full extension, flexion within 10 degrees of other side |
| Quadriceps activation | No visible inhibition during terminal extension drills |
| Strength | Improving trend over two consecutive checks |
| Movement quality | Controlled single-leg squat without valgus collapse |
| Next-day response | No increase in swelling or limp |

If effusion increased after a session, the next session reduced volume or intensity. If effusion remained stable, training load could progress.

### Load Management Algorithm

The fictional algorithm used four response categories:

| Response Category | Definition | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Green | No swelling increase, pain ≤ 2/10 | Progress load 5-10% |
| Yellow | Mild swelling increase or pain 3/10 | Repeat previous load |
| Orange | Effusion grade increases or pain 4-5/10 | Reduce load 20-30% |
| Red | Sharp pain, locking, instability, marked swelling | Stop progression and clinical review |

This table is useful for chunking tests because it contains short, dense decision rules.

## Safety and Clinical Escalation

### Adverse Events

No graft ruptures occurred in the synthetic dataset. No infections were reported. The most common rehabilitation-related issues were transient swelling, anterior knee pain, delayed-onset muscle soreness, and fear during landing drills.

| Event | Standard Pathway | Swelling-Guided Pathway |
|---|---:|---:|
| Transient anterior knee pain | 31% | 28% |
| Hamstring soreness | 18% | 20% |
| Effusion flare | 41% | 23% |
| Temporary training pause | 17% | 22% |
| Additional imaging referral | 12% | 10% |

The swelling-guided pathway had more temporary training pauses but fewer effusion flares. This is not a contradiction. Pauses were used proactively to prevent symptom escalation.

### Red-Flag Symptoms

Participants were referred for clinical reassessment if they had:
- locked knee,
- progressive extension loss,
- new giving-way episode,
- sudden sharp joint-line pain,
- large effusion after minor activity,
- fever or wound concerns,
- suspected graft failure.

This section is useful for retrieval questions because it differentiates routine swelling management from symptoms requiring medical reassessment.

## Mechanistic Interpretation

### Why Effusion Matters

Joint effusion may inhibit quadriceps activation. Even small effusion can alter motor output, reduce confidence, and change movement strategy. A patient may compensate by shifting weight to the non-operated limb during squats, jumping, or landing.

### Why Time Alone Is Insufficient

Time since surgery does not guarantee tissue readiness, neuromuscular control, or strength symmetry. Two patients at six months post-operation can have very different swelling, extension, strength, and movement quality. A fixed calendar progression may therefore be too aggressive for one person and too conservative for another.

### Why Criteria-Based Progression Helped

The swelling-guided pathway linked training progression to response. If symptoms increased, load was reduced. If symptoms remained stable, load progressed. This approach may have allowed more consistent training exposure over time, even when short-term progression was slower.

## Discussion

The fictional results support criteria-based rehabilitation for patients with persistent effusion after ACL reconstruction. The approach improved quadriceps symmetry and reduced flare frequency. It did not necessarily accelerate return to running. Instead, it reduced regression after clearance.

### Important Nuances

A retrieval system should preserve these distinctions:
- The swelling-guided group had later median return-to-run clearance but fewer regressions.
- More temporary pauses occurred in the swelling-guided group, but fewer effusion flares occurred.
- The primary outcome was quadriceps LSI, not return-to-run timing.
- MRI was used selectively, not routinely.
- The study did not prove that every swollen knee after ACL reconstruction needs imaging.
- The study did not prove that 90% LSI alone is enough for return to sport.
